Understanding Diabetic Retinopathy Categories

Diabetic retinopathy progresses through different stages of severity. Early detection and proper treatment at each stage can help preserve your vision. Learn about each category and the recommended actions below.

No DR (Diabetic Retinopathy)

No signs of diabetic retinopathy detected. The retina appears healthy with no visible damage from diabetes.

Recommended Steps:
  • Continue regular eye exams annually
  • Maintain optimal blood sugar control
  • Monitor blood pressure and cholesterol
  • Follow a healthy diet and exercise routine
  • Take prescribed diabetes medications as directed

Mild Diabetic Retinopathy

Small areas of swelling in the retina's blood vessels (microaneurysms). Early stage with minimal symptoms.

Recommended Steps:
  • Schedule eye exams every 6-12 months
  • Improve diabetes management immediately
  • Control blood pressure (target <140/90)
  • Consider diabetes education classes
  • Discuss medication adjustments with doctor
  • Implement lifestyle changes

Moderate Diabetic Retinopathy

Blood vessels are blocked, depriving areas of the retina of blood supply. May cause vision changes.

Recommended Steps:
  • Eye exams every 3-6 months
  • Immediate diabetes management optimization
  • Consider referral to retinal specialist
  • Monitor for vision changes daily
  • Strict blood pressure control
  • Consider anti-VEGF injections if needed

Severe Diabetic Retinopathy

Many blood vessels are blocked, causing significant areas of retina to lose blood supply. High risk of vision loss.

Immediate Actions Required:
  • Urgent referral to retinal specialist
  • Eye exams every 2-3 months
  • Consider laser photocoagulation
  • Anti-VEGF injections may be needed
  • Aggressive diabetes control
  • Monitor for proliferative changes

Proliferative Diabetic Retinopathy

Most advanced stage. New blood vessels grow in the retina and vitreous, which can bleed and cause severe vision loss or blindness.

Emergency Treatment Required:
  • Immediate ophthalmologist consultation
  • Urgent laser surgery (panretinal photocoagulation)
  • Anti-VEGF injections
  • Consider vitrectomy if needed
  • Monthly follow-up appointments
  • Aggressive systemic control
  • Monitor for complications
  • Vision rehabilitation if needed
